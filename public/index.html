Solitaire, also known as Patience, has captivated card game enthusiasts for over two centuries. The game's origins trace back to 18th century Europe, where it was first documented in Germany and Scandinavia as a single-player card game designed to pass time and test one's patience.

Klondike Solitaire, the most popular variant, gained its name during the Klondike Gold Rush of the 1890s. Prospectors and miners in Canada's Yukon Territory would play this challenging card game during long, cold nights, making it synonymous with the gold rush era.

The digital revolution transformed solitaire forever when Microsoft included it with Windows 3.0 in 1990. This move introduced millions to computer gaming and made Klondike Solitaire one of the most played video games in history. The simple mouse-based gameplay helped users learn computer navigation while enjoying a familiar card game.

Today, online solitaire continues this legacy, offering the same addictive gameplay that has entertained players for generations. Whether you're looking to improve concentration, pass time, or simply enjoy a classic card game, solitaire remains the perfect digital patience game for players of all ages.

The rules are elegantly simple: build foundation piles from Ace to King in each suit, while using the tableau columns to organize cards in descending order with alternating colors. This timeless formula has made solitaire a beloved classic across cultures and generations.

Game Setup

Klondike Solitaire uses a standard 52-card deck. Cards are dealt into seven tableau columns: the first column has 1 card (face-up), the second has 2 cards (1 face-down, 1 face-up), continuing this pattern until the seventh column has 7 cards (6 face-down, 1 face-up). Remaining cards form the stock pile.

Objective

Move all cards to four foundation piles, one for each suit (♠ ♥ ♦ ♣). Each foundation must be built in ascending order from Ace to King in the same suit.

Gameplay Rules

  • Tableau Building: Build down in alternating colors (red on black, black on red). For example, a red 6 can go on a black 7.
  • Moving Cards: You can move face-up cards between tableau columns. Multiple cards can be moved together if they form a proper sequence.
  • Revealing Cards: When you move a face-up card, flip the face-down card beneath it.
  • Empty Columns: Only Kings can be placed in empty tableau columns.
  • Stock and Waste: Draw cards from the stock pile to the waste pile. The top waste card can be played to tableau or foundations.
  • Foundations: Build foundations up from Ace to King in suit. Once placed, cards typically can't be moved back to tableau.

Winning Strategy Tips

Always prioritize revealing face-down cards and creating empty tableau columns. Move cards to foundations when it doesn't limit your options. Think several moves ahead before making decisions, and don't rush to move cards to foundations if they might be needed in the tableau.
